Tomato Growing Guide

Complete Guide to Growing Tomatoes

Follow these simple steps to grow delicious tomatoes in your urban garden, balcony, or windowsill.

Step 1: Starting Seeds

Start your tomato plants from seeds indoors 6-8 weeks before the last expected frost in your area.

What You'll Need:

  • Tomato seeds
  • Seed-starting containers with drainage holes
  • Seed-starting mix
  • Spray bottle for watering
  • Bright light source or grow lights

Instructions:

  1. Fill containers with seed-starting mix, leaving ½ inch of space at the top
  2. Plant seeds ¼ inch deep, 2-3 seeds per container
  3. Gently water using a spray bottle to avoid displacing seeds
  4. Cover with plastic wrap to maintain moisture (remove once sprouts appear)
  5. Place in a warm location (70-80°F / 21-27°C)
  6. Once seedlings emerge, provide 14-16 hours of light daily
  7. Water when soil surface feels dry
  8. After seedlings develop their first true leaves, thin to one plant per container

Step 2: Container Selection & Preparation

Choose the right containers and prepare them for your tomato plants.

What You'll Need:

  • 5-gallon (19L) container or larger per plant
  • High-quality potting mix
  • Compost
  • Organic fertilizer (check local regulations)
  • Crushed eggshells (optional, to prevent blossom end rot)

Container Requirements:

  • Minimum 5-gallon (19L) capacity
  • Good drainage holes
  • Sturdy material that won't tip over as plants grow

Soil Preparation:

  1. Mix 75% high-quality potting mix with 25% compost
  2. Add organic fertilizer according to package directions
  3. Mix in a handful of crushed eggshells if available
  4. Fill container, leaving 2 inches of space at the top

Step 3: Transplanting

Move your seedlings to their final growing containers when they're ready.

When to Transplant:

  • Seedlings are 6-8 inches tall
  • Have developed a strong stem and several sets of true leaves
  • Outdoor temperatures remain above 50°F (10°C) at night
  • All danger of frost has passed

Hardening Off Process:

Before transplanting outdoors, gradually acclimate seedlings to outdoor conditions:

  1. Day 1-2: Place seedlings outside in a sheltered, shady spot for 2-3 hours
  2. Day 3-4: Increase time outside to 4-5 hours, introducing some morning sun
  3. Day 5-6: Extend time to 6-8 hours with more sun exposure
  4. Day 7: Leave plants out all day, including some direct sun exposure

Transplanting Steps:

  1. Water seedlings thoroughly a few hours before transplanting
  2. Make a hole in the prepared container soil
  3. Remove lower leaves from seedling stem
  4. Plant deeply—bury the stem up to the remaining leaves (tomatoes will form roots along the buried stem)
  5. Gently firm soil around the plant
  6. Water thoroughly after transplanting

Step 4: Support & Training

Provide support for your plants to keep fruit off the ground and improve air circulation.

Support Options:

  • Tomato cages: Simple, convenient, good for determinate varieties
  • Stakes: Inexpensive, requires regular tying of stems
  • Trellises: Great for indeterminate varieties in larger containers
  • String systems: Efficient use of vertical space

Installation Tips:

  • Install supports at planting time to avoid root damage
  • Place support deep enough in soil to remain stable as plants grow
  • For stakes, position them 2-3 inches away from stem

Training Plants:

  1. As plants grow, gently tie stems to supports using soft garden ties
  2. Secure the main stem every 8-10 inches of growth
  3. Allow enough slack in ties to prevent stem damage as plants thicken
  4. Check and adjust ties regularly throughout the growing season

Step 5: Care & Maintenance

Provide proper care to ensure healthy plants and abundant harvests.

Watering:

  • Water deeply when the top inch of soil feels dry
  • Containers may need daily watering during hot weather
  • Always water at the base of plants to keep foliage dry
  • Add a layer of mulch to retain moisture

Fertilizing:

  • Apply balanced organic fertilizer every 3-4 weeks
  • Once plants start flowering, switch to a fertilizer higher in phosphorus and potassium
  • Follow package directions—over-fertilizing can reduce fruit production

Pruning:

  • Remove suckers (shoots that grow in the crotch between branches)
  • Prune lower leaves that touch the soil
  • For indeterminate varieties, remove some foliage to improve air circulation
  • Use clean scissors or pruners to avoid disease transmission

Pest & Disease Management:

  • Inspect plants regularly for signs of pests or disease
  • Use universally approved methods: insecticidal soap, horticultural oil, or diatomaceous earth
  • Companion planting with basil, marigolds, or nasturtiums can deter pests naturally
  • Remove diseased leaves promptly
  • Avoid overhead watering to prevent fungal issues
  • For serious infestations, consult local garden centers for regionally-approved treatments

Step 6: Harvesting & Enjoying

Enjoy the fruits of your labor by harvesting and using your homegrown tomatoes.

When to Harvest:

  • Harvest when fruits have developed full color
  • Fruits should be firm but yield slightly to gentle pressure
  • Ripe tomatoes should detach easily from the vine

Harvesting Technique:

  1. Hold the tomato in one hand and the stem in the other
  2. Gently twist or use scissors to cut the stem just above the fruit
  3. Harvest in the morning for best flavor
  4. Check plants every 1-2 days during peak season

Storage Tips:

  • For best flavor, store at room temperature, not in the refrigerator
  • Place stem-side down on a flat surface
  • Keep out of direct sunlight
  • Use within 4-7 days for optimal flavor

Enjoying Your Harvest:

  • Fresh in salads and sandwiches
  • Make homemade sauce or salsa
  • Share with neighbors and friends
  • Save seeds from heirloom varieties for next season
